# Judging Criteria — FOSSHack Jabalpur 2026

This document outlines the evaluation framework used by the jury to assess projects submitted for FOSSHack Jabalpur 2026. Participants are encouraged to review these criteria carefully before and during development.

---

## Evaluation Philosophy

FOSSHack is not a 24-hour sprint. Projects are evaluated based on the **complete development journey** over the month of March 2026, not solely on the final demonstration. Judges will review your GitHub repository history, code quality, documentation, and your project's real-world relevance. Projects will be evaluated for **novelty**, **completeness**, and **relevance**. After multiple rounds of elimination, results will be shared on **May 4, 2026**.

---

## Scoring Criteria

Projects will be evaluated across the following seven criteria. Each criterion carries equal weight in the overall evaluation.

---

### 1. GitHub Activity and Consistency

**What is evaluated:**

- Regularity and distribution of commits throughout the event period (March 1–31).
- Evidence of iterative development rather than bulk uploads near the deadline.
- Meaningful commit messages that reflect actual progress.
- Use of branches, pull requests, and issues where appropriate.

**What to avoid:**

- A single large commit at the end of the month.
- Vague or uninformative commit messages.
- Backdated or artificially created commit history.

> **Tip:** Make regular commits starting from March 1st. Avoid pushing everything on the last day.

---

### 2. Code Quality

**What is evaluated:**

- Readability — Is the code well-structured and consistently formatted?
- Modularity — Is the codebase organized into logical components or modules?
- Maintainability — Could another developer understand and build on this code?
- Absence of obvious bugs, security vulnerabilities, or deprecated practices.
- Appropriate use of libraries and dependencies.

**What to avoid:**

- Monolithic, uncommented, or difficult-to-follow code.
- Hardcoded credentials, API keys, or sensitive information.
- Excessive use of proprietary or closed-source dependencies.

---

### 3. Documentation

**What is evaluated:**

- Quality of the project README — including purpose, installation, usage, and contribution instructions.
- Presence of inline comments for complex logic.
- Any additional supporting documentation (API docs, architecture diagrams, etc.).
- Clarity and completeness of the demo video (maximum 3 minutes).

**What to avoid:**

- An absent or minimal README.
- Documentation that does not reflect the actual state of the project.

> **Tip:** Maintain proper documentation — a detailed README is crucial for evaluation.

---

### 4. Problem-Solving Impact

**What is evaluated:**

- Clear identification of the problem being solved.
- Relevance and real-world applicability of the solution.
- Evidence that the solution addresses the problem meaningfully.
- Potential for adoption or further development beyond the hackathon.

**What to avoid:**

- Projects that solve a trivial or artificial problem with no practical value.
- Vague or poorly defined problem statements.

> **Tip:** Focus on solving a real problem effectively rather than using hype technologies unnecessarily.

---

### 5. Completeness

**What is evaluated:**

- Does the project function as described in the documentation?
- Are the core features implemented and demonstrable?
- Is the project in a usable state at the time of submission?

**What to avoid:**

- Projects that are entirely conceptual with no working implementation.
- Submissions where core features are non-functional during the demo.

> **Tip:** Deploy your project so judges can easily test it.

---

### 6. Valid FOSS License

**What is evaluated:**

- Presence of an approved open-source license in the project repository.
- The license must be one of the options listed in the [LICENSES/](LICENSES/) folder, or another OSI-approved license.
- The license must be correctly applied (LICENSE file present, copyright header where applicable).

**What to avoid:**

- Projects with no license file.
- Use of proprietary, restrictive, or custom non-OSI-approved licenses.

---

### 7. Demo Video

**What is evaluated:**

- A pre-recorded video of maximum **3 minutes** demonstrating the project.
- The video must show the working application, not just slides or diagrams.
- Clear narration or on-screen text explaining what the project does.
- Demonstration of core features within the time limit.

**What to avoid:**

- Videos exceeding the 3-minute limit.
- Slide-only presentations without live demonstration.
- Poor audio or video quality that makes the demonstration unclear.

---

## It's Not Just Code

Remember that FOSS Hack welcomes contributions beyond code. Judges also value:

- **Documentation** — Guides, tutorials, READMEs, or API references for open-source projects.
- **Design** — UX/UI improvements, logos, or design systems.
- **Data** — Datasets, data pipelines, or data analysis contributions.

If contributing to existing projects, ensure your PRs are meaningful and follow the project's guidelines.

---

## Disqualification Criteria

Projects will be immediately disqualified for:

- Plagiarism or submission of pre-built work from outside the event period.
- Commits made entirely outside the March 1–31 window.
- Use of a proprietary or non-FOSS license.
- Violation of the [Code of Conduct](CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md).
- Projects built on Crypto, Web3, or Blockchain technologies.
- Core functionality dependent on proprietary APIs.

---

## Jury

The jury panel consists of members from the open-source community, FOSS United, and academic professionals. All jury decisions are final. The organizing team will not intervene in scoring or ranking decisions made by the jury. The cash prize will be split among the winners at the discretion of the jury.

---

## Results and Awards

- Results will be announced on **May 4, 2026**, after multiple rounds of elimination.
- Winners will be recognized as part of the national FOSSHack 2026 results.
